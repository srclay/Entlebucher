Getting Started

Join Our Puppy Waiting List

Our puppy list is open to new joiners who would like either a UK-born puppy, or perhaps to import from one of the reputable European breeders we have good contacts with.

If you're interested in the breed but would like more information before committing, why not contact us to arrange a meeting with an Entlebucher and their owner? Club members are always happy to meet prospective owners to talk about the breed and answer any questions.

Before applying, please consider: Entlebuchers develop a very strong bond with their principal carer and appreciate human company. They are not suited to being left alone for long periods. They enjoy free-running and, as intelligent working dogs, thrive on agility trials and flyball. The Club reserves the right to make full enquiries and visit the future home to assess suitability.

Puppy Care Guide

Raising Your Entlebucher

Tips on diet, training, exercise and more to give your new puppy the best start in life.

01

Diet & Nutrition

Your puppy will likely have been fed 4 meals a day since weaning — continue this until 12–14 weeks old, then reduce to 3 meals until 6 months, and 2 meals from 6 months onwards. Always follow the manufacturer's guidelines for age and weight. A good test: you should be able to feel but not see their ribs. Complete pet foods are most beneficial; some human food can be toxic to dogs. Fresh water should always be available.

02

Chewing & Teething

Teething is uncomfortable, so a solid chew is invaluable. At the first sign of chewing your arm or furniture, quickly substitute a chew — they'll learn fast. First teeth are usually lost at around 3–4 months; don't worry if you don't find them. Offering chews throughout their life keeps teeth clean and breath fresh. Bones are not advisable.

03

Sleeping

Provide a bed in a quiet area where they can feel safe and retreat from activity — this should be respected. Everything is new to them, so avoid bombarding them with too many experiences at once. Puppies suddenly decide they're tired and need a sleep. Please leave them; waking them for visitors will upset them and make them miserable.

04

Training

Basic training starts from day one. Training classes are excellent for socialisation — Entlebuchers can be cautious of strangers, so early exposure is key. Take it one step at a time; the first things to learn are their name and "no!" Be consistent: don't allow anything on day one that you wouldn't want at a year old. Never play tug of war with puppies.

05

Toilet Training

Take your puppy outside first thing in the morning, after each meal, after play, and before bed — hourly if you're at home. Watch for circling or nose-to-ground sniffing. Use the same door each time so they learn to wait there. Pick a single word for their eliminations and stick to it. Entlebuchers are usually quick to house train.

06

Exercise

During the first year, most energy goes into growing. Long walks are unnecessary — play is often sufficient. From 6 months to a year, a 30-minute walk is plenty. Protect soft bones and joints: don't let your puppy use stairs, jump from the car, or start agility until at least 12 months. Mental stimulation — hiding toys, new games — can tire them as much as physical exercise.

Safety

Car Travel

For the safety of both dog and passengers, and to comply with the Highway Code, your Entlebucher should travel in a crate or behind a dog guard in the boot, or on a seat secured with a dog seatbelt. These are inexpensive and easily attached to the dog's harness.

Important: Seatbelts are not suitable for use with a collar as a sudden stop will jerk their neck. Always use a harness.
